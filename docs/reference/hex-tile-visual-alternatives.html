Hegemony board UI study

One hex, too many jobs

The map needs to show terrain, resource type, yield amount, building slot capacity, and settlement ownership on a tiny tile. Other games solve this by refusing to make every detail equally loud.

Map first

Answer who is here and what the tile produces at a glance.

Inspector second

Move exact buildings, pops, and split-income math into the selected tile panel.

Fixed grammar

Give each data type a permanent physical zone on the hex.

How other games solve this

  • 1

    Move pieces off the hex.Catan uses intersections and edges, so the terrain tile can stay mostly economic.

  • 2

    Make territory ownership the loudest layer.Area-control games let pieces occupy an area, while detailed state lives elsewhere.

  • 3

    Stack and summarize.Risk-style armies, Eclipse fleets, and large 4X boards often use stacks plus count markers.

  • 4

    Use printed slots.Worker-placement and city boards reserve spaces, so capacity is visible without text.

  • 5

    Lean on selection.Digital 4X games keep the map clean and reveal the full tile card only when selected.

Recommended direction

Use a hybrid of fixed player seats, a hard economy plate, and an expanded selected-tile inspector.

In Hegemony terms: city/capital owns the center, colonies occupy reserved player positions or colony docks, yield and slots become small permanent badges, and buildings/pops stop fighting for map space.

Shape means kind Circle for city/capital, rounded square for colony, tiny squares for building slots.
Color means owner Player color is reserved for ownership only, not for resource or terrain.
Top means economy Resource type and yield live in one fixed plate so players never hunt for it.
Panel means detail Exact buildings, population, split income, and legality belong in the tile inspector.

Decision matrix

What I would actually ship

For the current rules where a tile can have at most two colonies and cities/capitals exclude colonies, the docked colony layout is the easiest immediate fix. If you expect future multi-presence or more contested states, fixed player seats age better.

Alternative Best at Weakness Hegemony fit
Fixed player seats Showing exactly which players are present, even in contested tiles. Needs players to learn the seat positions. Best long-term grammar.
Colony docks Supporting the current max-two-colonies rule with minimal clutter. Breaks if more than two settlements can share a tile later. Best immediate implementation.
Owner ring Map-scale ownership scanning. Weak at settlement type unless paired with badges. Good secondary layer.
Stacked totem Many pieces on one territory. Can hide exact ownership under the top chip. Use only if crowding grows beyond current rules.
Selection blow-up Full detail without overloading the map. Does not solve at-a-glance reading by itself. Should exist no matter which map grammar wins.
Printed slot sockets Making building capacity obvious without text. Consumes valuable space on low-zoom tiles. Great if buildings become a primary map concern.
