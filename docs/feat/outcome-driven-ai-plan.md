# Outcome-driven AI plan

Implementation plan for replacing fixed feature prices with goal-directed, sampled planning and
for evolving the simulation policy into a fair player-facing CPU.

- **Status:** approved direction; implementation not started
- **Date:** 2026-07-23
- **Primary policy:** a new outcome-driven policy, developed beside the existing `master`
- **Related audit:** [AI bot parity and vision audit](ai-bot-parity.md)
- **Current implementation:** [`src/sim/policies.ts`](../../src/sim/policies.ts)

## 1. Decision

Build the AI in this order:

1. Define a non-cheating player observation.
2. Replace scalar feature pricing with a hierarchical outcome vector and named plans.
3. Add grounded estimates for victory progress, collapse risk, plan success, and plan completion time.
4. Add multi-round sampled rollouts using the current `master` and specialist policies as continuation policies.
5. Validate the rollouts through capability scenarios and paired tournaments.
6. Turn the validated rollout system into bounded Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).
7. Add learned win/rank prediction only after the search, observation, telemetry, and evaluation protocols are stable.

Do **not** begin by training a neural policy, replacing the current weights with another large weight
table, or treating games generated by one policy as ground truth.

The existing `master` remains useful as:

- an action-ordering prior;
- a rollout/default policy;
- a leaf evaluator while the new outcome model is incomplete;
- a frozen baseline in tournaments;
- a fallback when the CPU exhausts its decision budget.

It stops being the authoritative definition of a good position.

## 2. Why this change is required

The current evaluator assigns fixed prices to unrelated game objects:

```text
victory card                   120
city                           60
colony                         30
citizen / freeman / slave      30 / 20 / 12
available building slot         4
useful Gymnasion synergy        30
stored influence                2 per unit
reachable frontier yield        2 per printed yield
```

These numbers make action comparison possible, but they encode assumptions rather than measured
outcomes. They can:

- double-count the same benefit;
- value an asset even when it serves no current victory route;
- optimize short-term gains that create later collapse;
- keep a resource's value constant when the player's need for it changes;
- reward building or promotion without considering rival responses;
- become more damaging when deeper search optimizes the wrong value more effectively.

The beam-vs-Smart results already demonstrate the last failure: deeper search amplified the
evaluator's over-promotion behavior. More search does not repair a bad objective.

No decision system is free of preferences. Choosing “win before economy” is itself a design choice.
The goal is not to eliminate judgment; it is to express judgment in terms of game outcomes and
ordered priorities instead of arbitrary exchange rates between every object.

## 3. Required outcome hierarchy

Every normal-play, setup, event, riot, chance, and Assembly decision must use the same ordered model.

1. **Win immediately if possible.**
2. **Prevent an opponent's immediate victory.**
3. **Avoid collapse:** riots, starvation, forced population loss, or other state failure.
4. **Maximize expected progress toward the next victory condition.**
5. **Minimize expected turns required to execute the current plan.**
6. **Use resources and income only as inputs to those plans.**

This changes the question from:

```text
Is one city worth 60 and one citizen worth 30?
```

to:

```text
How much does this city reduce my expected turns to victory?
How often does it survive the intervening rival turns?
Which victory routes does it unlock or stabilize?
How often does a rival take the required location first?
What collapse risk does the city, its population, or its upkeep introduce?
```

Resources must also be grounded in a plan:

```text
wood value      → change in turns until the intended colony or building
stone value     → change in turns until the intended city or civic building
gold value      → change in plan affordability, flexibility, or chance exposure
food value      → change in sustainable growth, shortage risk, and plan runway
influence value → change in probability of winning an important Assembly outcome
frontier value  → change in viable expansion routes and victory routes
```

Risk tolerance, ranking preferences, difficulty, and personality will still differ between CPUs.
Those differences must operate on outcome distributions and plan selection, not by giving every game
object a different unexplained price.

### 3.1 Engine-specific meaning of “win immediately”

Hegemony normally awards victory at the start of the player's next turn after the player holds enough
victory cards. The outcome model therefore distinguishes:

1. **Terminal win:** the game is already over with this seat as `winner`, including a Stratokles coup.
2. **Secured next-turn win:** the player can end the current turn holding enough cards and sampled or
   enumerated rival replies cannot break the winning set before the next turn starts.
3. **Fragile next-turn win:** the player currently holds enough cards but one or more rival replies can
   remove a card.

Priority 1 selects a terminal or secured win. A fragile winning set must be evaluated against the full
intervening table, not treated as a banked victory card count.

### 3.2 Meaning of “prevent an opponent's immediate victory”

For each rival, the planner must identify:

- a terminal coup or other direct win available now;
- enough victory cards to win at that rival's next turn start;
- one-move or one-Assembly-item card swings that create such a set;
- actions that remove one of the AI's cards while adding one to the rival;
- map, patronage, population, happiness, stockpile, citizen, or city races that are currently pivotal.

The threat value is the probability that **any** rival wins before the acting player has another turn.
The highest individual threat is retained in the explanation trace.

### 3.3 Meaning of “avoid collapse”

Collapse is not a generic negative resource score. It is a sampled or exact event with a probability
and consequence. At minimum it includes:

- entering a riot or revolt tier;
- starvation/food-deficit escalation;
- forced population loss;
- an unaffordable or unsustainable settlement plan;
- income suppression that breaks the active plan;
- forced loss of a victory card caused by the collapse;
- action-cap or rules deadlock in simulations.

For each candidate action, record:

```text
P(riot before next own turn)
P(revolt before next own turn)
P(forced population loss within horizon)
expected population loss within horizon
P(active plan becomes infeasible)
P(victory route becomes infeasible)
```

Avoidance takes priority over ordinary progress, except when accepting the risk produces a terminal or
secured win.

### 3.4 Meaning of “victory progress”

Use [`victoryStandings`](../../src/game/victory.ts) as the authoritative source for all six cards:

- Polis Builder — cities;
- Demos — total population;
- Civic Elite — citizens;
- Treasurer — material stockpile;
- Beloved of the People — happiness;
- Voice of the Assembly — politician patronage, with authored-stelae tiebreak.

For every card and player, expose:

```ts
type CardProgress = {
  metric: VictoryMetric;
  currentValue: number;
  minimum: number;
  holder: PlayerId | null;
  distanceToMinimum: number;
  distanceToSoleLead: number;
  leadMargin: number;
  canFlipWithinHorizon: boolean;
  holdProbabilityAtNextOwnTurn: number;
  expectedTurnsToAcquire: number | null;
  expectedTurnsToLose: number | null;
};
```

Progress is not the raw metric alone. A city matters when it:

- reaches the minimum;
- breaks a tie;
- creates a sole lead;
- stabilizes a held card;
- contributes to a combination reaching `cardsToWin`;
- denies the corresponding card or winning set to a rival.

The planner must consider victory **routes**, not six isolated gradients. A route is a plausible set of
cards and plans that reaches `ruleset.victory.cardsToWin`.

```ts
type VictoryRoute = {
  cardIds: string[];
  activePlans: PlanId[];
  completionProbability: number;
  expectedTurns: number | null;
  rivalInterferenceProbability: number;
  collapseProbability: number;
};
```

### 3.5 Meaning of “current plan”

The policy needs durable intent across decisions and turns. A plan is not a fixed action script; it is a
goal with prerequisites, candidate steps, costs, expected completion, and abandonment conditions.

```ts
type Plan = {
  id: PlanId;
  kind:
    | "secureVictory"
    | "denyVictory"
    | "stabilize"
    | "foundColony"
    | "upgradeCity"
    | "build"
    | "grow"
    | "promote"
    | "relocate"
    | "pursuePatronage"
    | "passResolution"
    | "repealResolution"
    | "pursueStratokles";
  targetPlayer?: PlayerId;
  targetTileId?: string;
  targetCardId?: string;
  targetBuildingId?: string;
  targetPoliticianId?: string;
  prerequisites: PlanCondition[];
  candidateSteps: LegalMoveTemplate[];
  resourceBudget: Partial<Resources>;
  expectedTurns: number | null;
  completionProbability: number;
  collapseProbability: number;
  victoryEffects: CardProgressDelta[];
  abandonWhen: PlanCondition[];
};
```

Plans must be regenerated or invalidated when:

- a target tile is taken;
- a relevant Law changes cost or income;
- an event or riot changes resources/population;
- a victory-card holder or minimum race changes;
- an Assembly result changes political position;
- expected completion becomes worse than another route;
- the plan becomes unaffordable, unsafe, or irrelevant.

Ending a turn can be a positive plan step. The planner must be able to say, “save now to upgrade next
turn,” rather than requiring every chosen action to increase the current state's scalar score.

## 4. Outcome vector and comparison contract

Do not immediately collapse the hierarchy into one number. Return a structured vector:

```ts
type OutcomeVector = {
  terminalWin: boolean;
  securedWinProbability: number;
  opponentWinProbabilityBeforeNextTurn: number;
  collapseProbability: number;
  expectedForcedPopLoss: number;
  victoryRouteCompletionProbability: number;
  expectedVictoryCardsAtNextOwnTurn: number;
  expectedTurnsToVictory: number | null;
  activePlanCompletionProbability: number;
  expectedTurnsToPlan: number | null;
  terminalWinProbability: number;
  expectedTerminalRank: number;
};
```

Compare candidates lexicographically:

1. terminal win;
2. secured-win probability;
3. lower opponent-win probability before the next own turn;
4. lower collapse probability and forced loss;
5. higher victory-route completion probability;
6. higher stable card progress;
7. lower expected turns to victory;
8. higher active-plan completion probability;
9. lower expected turns to the active plan;
10. terminal win probability and expected rank from longer rollouts.

### 4.1 Statistical ties

Sampled values are estimates. Do not prefer a candidate because of meaningless sampling noise.

- Preserve sample count, mean, variance, and confidence interval for every sampled field.
- Treat overlapping confidence intervals as unresolved at that priority.
- Move to the next hierarchy item only when the current item is statistically tied or operationally
  equivalent.
- If the decision budget expires while two candidates remain indistinguishable, select using a seeded
  tie-break among the near-equivalent set.

The equivalence rule is a search/timing parameter, not a permanent price assigned to a city or pop.

### 4.2 Terminal reward in multiplayer games

Use a per-player result vector rather than pretending the game is two-player zero-sum:

```text
winner:       terminalWin = 1
non-winners:  terminalWin = 0
```

Expected rank is a secondary statistic for unfinished/cutoff comparisons and CPU personality. It must
not allow “comfortable second place” to beat an available win.

## 5. Grounded resource and feature value

Resources do not receive global permanent values in the new policy. Their value is derived from plans
and sampled outcomes.

### 5.1 Resource shadow value

For an active plan and one additional unit of resource `r`:

```text
time value(r) = E[turns to plan before] - E[turns to plan after +1 r]

success value(r) =
  P(plan succeeds after +1 r) - P(plan succeeds before)

victory value(r) =
  P(victory route completes after +1 r) - P(victory route completes before)
```

The trace may report all three. There is no need to force them into a universal gold-equivalent value.

Consequences:

- Wood may be valuable while preparing a colony and nearly irrelevant after the required wood is secured.
- Stone may be valuable for a city route but not for a threatened Beloved/Voice route.
- Food can be negative when more growth would cause collapse and positive when it prevents starvation.
- Influence is valuable when it changes a pivotal Assembly probability, not merely because it is stored.
- A frontier tile is valuable when it belongs to a viable route and is likely to remain available.

### 5.2 Building, population, and settlement value

Buildings, pops, colonies, and cities use the same counterfactual method:

```text
feature value = outcome(after feature) - outcome(without feature)
```

The comparison must include:

- changes to victory-card margins and route combinations;
- future plan options unlocked;
- income and costs over their actual durations;
- collapse risk;
- opponent denial or interference;
- map access and target races;
- probability that the benefit survives until the next own turn.

The legacy evaluator may provide rollout guidance, but its fixed feature weights do not decide the
outcome-driven policy's final ordering.

## 6. Fair observation and belief state

Before adding rollouts, stop policies from receiving unrestricted `HegemonyState`.

```ts
type BotObservation = {
  publicState: PublicGameState;
  ownPrivateState: OwnPrivateState;
  legalMoves: LegalMove[];
  publicChanceModels: ChanceModel[];
  beliefState: BotBeliefState;
};
```

### 6.1 Must be visible

- board, settlements, populations, buildings, transfers, and legal reach;
- public resources if the human game exposes them;
- current income breakdown and timed-effect durations;
- victory standings, minimums, margins, and public tiebreaks;
- active Laws, Assembly phase, ballot, votes, bribes, veto availability, and politician standings;
- public deck/discard composition and counts where rules expose them;
- known event, riot, and venture outcome distributions;
- the player's own private cards or held resolution.

### 6.2 Must not be visible

- ordered future event/resolution decks;
- another player's private information;
- the next seeded riot, venture, or event result;
- hidden random state not inferable from public history.

### 6.3 Belief rules

- Sample hidden worlds consistent with the observation.
- Never rewrite beliefs from the engine's actual hidden answer.
- Store public history sufficient to update remaining distributions.
- Use a separate rollout RNG derived from the decision seed; never advance or inspect live `G.rng`.
- Make observation redaction and belief consistency testable independently of policy strength.

## 7. Rolling Horizon Monte Carlo — first planner

Implement sampled action evaluation before a full MCTS tree. This is the shortest path from the current
Master to cross-turn, opponent-aware, chance-aware decisions.

### 7.1 Algorithm

For every legal candidate action:

```text
for each candidate action A:
    repeat until rollout or time budget is exhausted:
        sample a hidden world consistent with the observation
        clone the state
        apply A through applyMove

        while game is not over and horizon is not reached:
            sample public random outcomes without reading the live future
            choose each seat's actions with a rollout policy
            advance through complete turns and Assembly sub-processes

        if game ended:
            record winner and terminal rank
        else:
            measure the hierarchical OutcomeVector at the leaf

    aggregate the candidate's outcome distribution

compare candidates through the ordered OutcomeVector
commit the selected action
re-plan after the action is applied
```

### 7.2 Horizon

The first implementation must cross at least:

- the acting player's end turn;
- every rival seat's response;
- the acting player's next turn opening, including victory, upkeep, income, arrivals, events, and unrest;
- any Assembly opened inside that interval.

That is the minimum required to distinguish a secured winning set from a fragile one and to support
intentional saving. Longer simulation profiles can extend to multiple complete rounds or terminal state.

### 7.3 Budgets

Use profiles rather than one hard-coded count:

| Profile      | Purpose                             | Budget behavior                                     |
| ------------ | ----------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| `test`       | deterministic unit/capability tests | fixed small rollout count                           |
| `batch`      | balance and policy tournaments      | fixed larger count; reproducible                    |
| `cpu-easy`   | player-facing low difficulty        | short time/count; more near-best variation          |
| `cpu-normal` | normal CPU                          | moderate bounded time/count                         |
| `cpu-hard`   | strongest shipped CPU               | larger bounded time/count; stronger opponent league |
| `analysis`   | offline investigation               | long or terminal rollouts                           |

Actual counts and milliseconds must be chosen from variance and latency benchmarks. They are compute
budgets, not semantic feature values.

### 7.4 Rollout policies

Do not use only one continuation policy. The initial rollout league contains:

- frozen `master`;
- `beam`;
- `political`;
- `smart`;
- `greedy`;
- noisy Master, which selects among statistically near-equivalent actions;
- risk-seeking and risk-averse outcome-policy variants once available;
- previous frozen versions of the outcome-driven policy.

Each rollout samples or assigns a plausible policy per rival seat. Reports record which policies were
used. This tests whether a candidate works only against one exploitable continuation.

### 7.5 Setup

The selected policy must control setup in outcome-driven and CPU games. Random uniform setup remains a
chaos/test option only.

Setup rollouts evaluate:

- initial production and food sustainability;
- population composition;
- nearby city anchors and multi-hop expansion corridors;
- rival setup/settlement races;
- access to distinct victory routes;
- resilience to plausible shuffled terrain and seat order.

## 8. From rollouts to MCTS

MCTS does not require an existing dataset of expert games. It performs online policy improvement using
the simulator. It needs:

- correct legal moves and state transitions — already present;
- public chance models and fair observations — Phase 1 below;
- a default/rollout policy — current Master and the rollout league;
- terminal winner data — already present;
- a leaf evaluation when simulations stop early — the outcome vector;
- a search budget and exploration rule.

After rolling-horizon evaluation is correct, reuse it in MCTS:

1. **Selection:** choose a branch using visit statistics, outcome vectors, and exploration.
2. **Expansion:** add a legal action or chance outcome.
3. **Simulation:** use the rollout league through full turns/rounds.
4. **Backpropagation:** update a result vector for every seat, not one zero-sum scalar.
5. **Decision:** choose the root action with the strongest supported outcome distribution.

### 8.1 Hegemony-specific MCTS requirements

- **Multi-action turns:** represent actions individually but preserve end-turn/round boundaries.
- **Multiple players:** store per-seat results; each simulated seat chooses for itself.
- **Chance:** sample from public distributions or use chance nodes where enumeration is small.
- **Hidden information:** search sampled information sets, not actual hidden engine state.
- **Large move families:** use action grouping, move ordering, and progressive widening where needed.
- **Assembly:** model the full multi-seat proposal/vote process, including pivotality, bribe cost, and veto.
- **Plans:** seed priors toward actions belonging to viable plans without making them mandatory.
- **Transpositions:** add only after observation/state hashing is correct and hidden-state safe.

Do not start MCTS until one-round sampled evaluation passes the behavioral and anti-peek gates. A tree
would otherwise multiply incorrect leaf values and unfair information access.

## 9. Synthetic data and the bootstrap loop

### 9.1 There is no initial-data blocker

The simulator itself generates transition and terminal-outcome data. MCTS is online search, not a model
that must first be trained on expert examples. The first useful loop is:

```text
frozen Master and specialist league
    ↓ supplies rollout actions
rolling-horizon policy
    ↓ supplies stronger decisions and trajectories
MCTS policy
    ↓ supplies better decisions and labeled positions
simple learned value model, if useful
    ↓ accelerates later MCTS
stronger MCTS policy
```

Each new policy must defeat frozen earlier policies; replacing all opponents at once hides regressions.

### 9.2 Prevent a synthetic feedback loop

Self-play data reflects the policies that created it. Apply all of these controls:

1. **Policy diversity:** use the rollout league, not clones of the current policy only.
2. **Frozen opponents:** retain every promoted milestone agent in evaluation leagues.
3. **Exploration:** sample near-equivalent moves and uncommon plans during data generation.
4. **Seat rotation:** evaluate every policy in every seat on the same seeds.
5. **Board diversity:** use Shuffled boards for representative campaigns and Classic only for fixed fixtures.
6. **Opening diversity:** include policy-controlled, randomized, and adversarial opening sets.
7. **State curriculum:** generate early-, mid-, late-game, Assembly, unrest, and near-victory positions.
8. **Holdout scenarios:** never tune directly on the complete capability suite.
9. **Frozen ruleset evaluation:** separate bot-strength experiments from game-balance changes.
10. **Human games later:** collect human choices and outcomes to measure human resemblance, not as a
    prerequisite for initial strength.

### 9.3 Strong play versus human-like play

Self-play can produce strong but unfamiliar behavior. Treat these as separate objectives:

- **Strength:** win probability, expected rank, denial, survival, and rules-correct planning.
- **Human likeness:** similarity to human action distributions, understandable plans, bounded calculation,
  plausible mistakes, and personality.

The first outcome-driven CPU targets strength, fairness, and explainability. Once real human games exist,
store observation, legal moves, chosen move, decision time, outcome, and optional plan label. Human data
can later:

- provide an action prior;
- identify plans humans use that self-play missed;
- calibrate easy/normal CPU mistakes;
- measure whether explanations match plausible intent.

Do not train directly on private player data without an explicit data policy and consent path.

## 10. Decision explanations and telemetry

Every outcome-driven choice must be inspectable.

```ts
type DecisionTrace = {
  gameSeed: number;
  turn: number;
  playerID: PlayerId;
  observationHash: string;
  legalMoveCount: number;
  selectedMove: LegalMove;
  selectedPlan: Plan | null;
  candidates: Array<{
    move: LegalMove;
    outcome: OutcomeEstimate;
    planEffects: PlanEffect[];
    reasonCodes: ReasonCode[];
    rolloutCount: number;
  }>;
  rolloutProfile: string;
  rolloutPolicies: Record<string, number>;
  elapsedMs: number;
};
```

Required reason-code families:

```text
WIN_NOW
SECURE_NEXT_TURN_WIN
BLOCK_RIVAL_WIN
REDUCE_RIOT_RISK
REDUCE_STARVATION_RISK
AVOID_FORCED_POP_LOSS
ADVANCE_VICTORY_CARD

```

Extend [`src/sim/telemetry.ts`](../../src/sim/telemetry.ts) with:

- move counts by policy and type;
- selected-plan counts, completion, abandonment, and duration;
- outcome-vector summaries at decision time;
- immediate and secured win opportunities taken/missed;

- collapse-risk changes and actual collapses;

- rollout counts, elapsed time, nodes, depth/round horizon, and cache hits;
- selected move's confidence relative to alternatives;
- policy and plan attribution for wins.

Decision traces are optional in large batches but must be enabled for capability tests, sampled audit games,
and CPU debugging.

## 11. Implementation phases

### Phase 0 — Freeze baselines and capability contract

**Deliverables**

- Preserve the existing `random`, `greedy`, `smart`, `beam`, `political`, and `master` policies.
- Record representative baseline tournaments on Shuffled boards with seat rotation.
- Add the feature/capability manifest required by the parity audit.
- Add move counts by type and per-policy behavior attribution.
- Store fixed regression seeds and canonical positions without treating their outcomes as tuning data.

**Exit gate**

- Every consequential move/feature has an observation, action, telemetry, and scenario-test entry.
- Existing policies replay deterministically.
- Baseline reports identify ruleset, content tune patch, policy version, seat assignment, and seed.

### Phase 1 — Observation and public chance APIs

**New modules**

- `src/ai/observation.ts` — `BotObservation`, redaction, public history.
- `src/ai/chance.ts` — public distributions for riot, venture, event, and resolution draws.
- `src/ai/belief.ts` — hidden-world sampling consistent with an observation.
- `src/ai/observation.test.ts` — anti-cheat and information-consistency fixtures.

**Changes**

- Update `Policy.choose` to accept a bot context/observation rather than unrestricted hidden state.
- Provide a temporary adapter for legacy policies using only permitted fields.
- Derive rollout RNG independently from live game RNG and bot tie-break RNG.

**Exit gate**

- A policy cannot read ordered future decks or actual next random results.
- Two engine states indistinguishable to a player produce equivalent observations.
- Sampling does not mutate or advance the live state or RNG.
- Public probabilities agree with the underlying outcome tables.

### Phase 2 — Outcome, victory-route, collapse, and plan primitives

**New modules**

- `src/ai/outcome.ts` — `OutcomeVector`, estimates, aggregation, comparison.
- `src/ai/victoryRoutes.ts` — card progress, margins, combinations, rival threats.
- `src/ai/collapse.ts` — unrest/starvation/forced-loss forecasts.
- `src/ai/plans.ts` — plan generation, progress, ETA, invalidation.
- `src/ai/explain.ts` — reason codes and decision traces.

**Policy**

- Register a new experimental policy ID such as `outcome`.
- Keep `master` unchanged as the legacy baseline until the new policy clears promotion gates.
- Use exact rules/counterfactuals where possible and sampled estimates where future responses matter.

**Exit gate**

- The outcome hierarchy passes all canonical behavior tests in Section 12.
- Resource shadow values change when the active plan changes.
- Ending the turn to save for a plan can beat a locally profitable action.
- No fixed city/pop/resource price decides the new policy's final action.
- Every selected action produces a reason code and plan/outcome trace.

### Phase 3 — Rolling Horizon Monte Carlo

**New modules**

- `src/ai/rollout.ts` — cloned multi-round simulation and aggregation.
- `src/ai/rolloutPolicies.ts` — frozen policy league and seat sampling.
- `src/ai/budgets.ts` — test, batch, CPU, and analysis profiles.
- `src/ai/rollout.test.ts` — determinism, horizon, opponent, and chance fixtures.

**Runner changes**

- Reuse legal moves and engine mutators.
- Add a rollout-safe runner that advances complete rounds and returns terminal/leaf results.
- Permit policy-controlled setup.
- Ensure rollouts can resolve the Assembly without sharing mutable deck/log state.

**Exit gate**

- Search crosses all rival responses and the next acting-player turn opening.
- Chance sampling covers every stochastic move family without peeking.
- At least one canonical scenario requires saving across turns and passes.
- At least one colony-race scenario changes choice based on rival arrival probability and passes.
- At least one Assembly scenario changes choice based on pass/pivotality probability and passes.
- Same observation, rollout seed, profile, and policy league produce the same decision trace.

### Phase 4 — Tournament promotion and policy iteration

**Deliverables**

- Rotated, paired-seed league runner.
- Frozen policy-version registry.
- Win/rank/collapse/behavior/runtime report with confidence intervals.
- Self-play trajectory export based on observations, not hidden engine state.
- A promoted `outcome` milestone only when Section 13 gates pass.

**Exit gate**

- The outcome policy improves finished-game results against the frozen league without increasing rules
  failures, action-cap hits, or collapse pathologies.
- Required feature behaviors remain covered.
- Gains hold across seats, seeds, Shuffled boards, and multiple rival policies.

### Phase 5 — MCTS

**New modules**

- `src/ai/mcts/tree.ts` — information-set-safe tree nodes and statistics.
- `src/ai/mcts/select.ts` — exploration and multi-player selection.
- `src/ai/mcts/expand.ts` — legal actions, chance, and progressive widening.
- `src/ai/mcts/backprop.ts` — per-seat outcome propagation.
- `src/ai/mcts/policy.ts` — registered `mcts` policy and fallback.

**Exit gate**

- With the same compute budget, MCTS performs at least as well as flat rolling-horizon evaluation in the
  frozen league and improves at least one long-combination capability.
- Strength increases or remains stable as the analysis budget increases.
- Search does not access hidden state, change live RNG, or violate deterministic replay.
- Decision latency and memory stay inside the selected CPU profile.

### Phase 6 — Player-facing CPU

**Deliverables**

- CPU controller integration using `enumerateLegalMoves → choose → applyMove`.
- Worker/background execution if profiling shows main-thread impact.
- Difficulty profiles based on rollout/search budget and opponent modeling.
- Seeded near-best variation.
- Plan/reason summaries suitable for developer UI and optional player-facing feedback.
- Save/replay support including AI policy version and decision seed.

**Exit gate**

- CPU never reads hidden information unavailable to a human seat.
- Turns complete within defined platform latency budgets.
- Easy/normal/hard exhibit ordered strength over statistically adequate campaigns.
- Lower difficulty makes bounded, plausible mistakes rather than illegal moves or deliberate self-destruction.

### Phase 7 — Learned value or policy model, optional

Begin only when Phases 0–6 produce trustworthy observation-based trajectories and promotion evidence.

Start with the smallest useful learned component:

1. Calibrated model predicting terminal win probability and expected rank from `BotObservation`.
2. Model predicting promising plans or action groups.
3. Use predictions to order MCTS expansion or replace expensive leaf rollouts.
4. Retain exact rules, MCTS verification, and frozen league evaluation.

Do not begin with an end-to-end AlphaZero clone. Promote a learned model only if it improves calibrated
outcome prediction, search strength per compute, or CPU latency without reducing capability coverage or
fairness.

## 12. Required behavioral scenarios

Create table-driven fixtures for at least the following. Each fixture asserts the selected hierarchy
reason, not only the exact move when multiple moves are equivalent.

### 12.1 Win and denial

- Take a terminal Stratokles win over all economic actions.
- End the turn when a winning card set is secured.
- Do not end when the card set is fragile and a defensive action can secure it.
- Block a rival's next-turn winning set even when the blocking move loses resources.
- Prefer a dual swing that removes a rival card and gains one over a one-sided gain.

### 12.2 Collapse

- Reject growth/promotion that crosses a riot/revolt threshold without a winning payoff.
- Buy or save for protection when expected forced loss exceeds the active plan's benefit.
- Accept collapse risk when it creates a terminal or statistically secured win.
- Recognize an expiring modifier instead of repeating today's bad income for six turns.

### 12.3 Plans and saving

- End the turn to afford a city upgrade next turn.
- Avoid spending a reserved resource on a weaker unrelated action.
- Abandon a city plan when a Law changes its cost beyond the horizon.
- Switch victory route when a rival takes the relevant sole lead.
- Value the same resource differently under two different active plans.

### 12.4 Map and population

- Choose a colony route by expected victory contribution, not summed neighboring yield.
- Account for a rival reaching the target first.
- Prefer a lower-yield chokepoint when it preserves the only viable route.
- Move population to seed a planned expansion or stabilize capacity.
- Reject an otherwise productive colony that is unsustainable or cannot support a route.

### 12.5 Assembly

- Evaluate a Law through its normal economic, victory, collapse, and map consequences.
- Vote based on whether the vote is pivotal, not only whether the resolution is favorable.
- Bribe/veto only when the action materially changes pass probability and outcome.
- Save influence for an identified future pivotal vote.
- Draw/propose Stratokles when it is the best comeback route; oppose it when a rival is positioned to win.
- Include spatial consequences when Master/outcome evaluates a resolution.

### 12.6 Chance and information

- Prefer the higher expected-value venture when distributions differ.
- Prefer insurance when its expected avoided loss justifies it under the hierarchy.
- Change behavior with public remaining-deck composition without reading order.
- Make the same decision from two states with identical observations but different hidden future order.
- Produce different sampled outcomes across rollout seeds while preserving replay for a fixed seed.

### 12.7 Setup

- Reject dominated placement/population combinations.
- Select a sustainable opening with at least one viable victory route.
- React to earlier seats' placements and contested corridors.
- Preserve opening diversity when several placements are statistically equivalent.

## 13. Tournament and promotion protocol

### 13.1 Baselines

Always include:

- frozen legacy `master`;
- frozen previous outcome/MCTS milestone;
- at least two specialist policies;
- a noisy/varied opponent policy;
- the candidate policy.

### 13.2 Match design

- Use identical seeds for candidate and baseline comparisons.
- Rotate every policy through every seat.
- Use Shuffled boards for representative strength claims.
- Run to real game termination with a high safety ceiling; report turn-cap share separately.
- Keep ruleset/content constant within a policy experiment.
- Record the full policy version, observation schema, rollout profile, and league composition.

### 13.3 Primary measures

- finished-game win rate by policy;
- expected terminal rank;
- victory reason and victory route;
- secured-win opportunities taken/missed;
- rival-win threats blocked/missed;
- collapse probability versus actual riot/revolt/population loss;
- policy strength by seat;
- decision latency, rollouts, nodes, and memory.

### 13.4 Promotion rule

Promote a candidate only when:

1. no parity/capability gate regresses;
2. no anti-cheat, RNG, legality, deadlock, or replay test fails;
3. its finished-game result against the frozen league improves with a reported confidence interval, or it
   achieves equivalent strength at materially lower compute;
4. the gain is not confined to one seat, seed range, board layout, or opponent;
5. collapse and force-end metrics do not show a new pathology;
6. representative decision traces show the intended hierarchy causing the change.

If evidence remains inconclusive after the predetermined campaign ceiling, record “inconclusive”; do not
claim improvement from the point estimate.

## 14. Difficulty and personality

Difficulty changes calculation quality:

- rollout/search budget;
- horizon;
- opponent-model diversity;
- chance sample count;
- plan-generation breadth;
- near-equivalent action randomness;
- quality of the leaf/value model.

Personality changes preferences **after** mandatory win/denial/fairness rules:

- risk tolerance over outcome variance;
- preferred victory routes when completion probabilities are similar;
- willingness to contest map or Assembly races;
- planning horizon and commitment/abandonment tendency;
- preference for stable versus high-variance near-equivalent plans.

Personality may not cause a CPU to ignore an available terminal win, knowingly permit an immediate rival
win without a compensating win, access hidden information, or choose illegal actions.

## 16. Risks and mitigations

| Risk                                             | Mitigation                                                                             |
| ------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Weak rollout policies distort results            | Diverse frozen league, exploration, iterative promotion, human data later              |
| Short horizon still favors immediate gains       | Cross full rival round first; increase horizon; MCTS; learned terminal predictor later |
| Compute grows with legal-move count              | Move grouping, pruning only by dominance, progressive widening, caching, profiles      |
| Search optimizes a faulty leaf outcome           | Hierarchical fixtures, terminal rollouts, explanation traces, frozen holdouts          |
| Hidden information leaks through clones          | Observation contract, information-set sampling, anti-cheat tests                       |
| Live RNG is peeked or consumed                   | Separate rollout RNG and distributions; invariant tests                                |
| Self-play converges to one exploitable style     | Policy league, frozen milestones, diverse openings, exploration                        |
| Strong play appears inhuman                      | Separate human-likeness objective, plan explanations, human traces later               |
| Personality reintroduces arbitrary weights       | Apply personality only to statistically near-equivalent outcome distributions          |
| Bot tuning contaminates game-balance conclusions | Freeze rules per policy test; promote bot before resuming balance campaigns            |
| MCTS is built before prerequisites               | Phase gates make observation and flat rollouts mandatory first                         |

## 17. Definition of done

The outcome-driven CPU program is complete when all of the following are true:

- Policies operate on a tested non-cheating observation rather than raw hidden state.
- The cumulative policy implements the six-level outcome hierarchy.
- Immediate/secured wins and rival immediate-win threats are searched through intervening turns.
- Collapse is represented by probability and consequence, not a flat happiness/resource penalty.
- Every victory card has shared progress, margin, stability, denial, and ETA queries.
- The bot creates, follows, saves for, revises, and abandons named multi-turn plans.
- Resources, buildings, populations, settlements, and map frontiers derive value from plans and outcome
  changes rather than permanent feature prices.
- Setup, ordinary turns, events, riots, chance actions, and Assembly use the same outcome contract.
- Random outcomes are evaluated from public distributions without peeking at live future RNG/deck order.
- Sampled planning includes rival turns and at least the next acting-player turn opening.
- A diverse frozen policy league prevents one-policy synthetic feedback.
- Decision traces explain the selected plan, outcome hierarchy level, sampled evidence, and alternatives.
- Canonical capability scenarios cover every game feature and all behaviors in Section 12.
- Paired, rotated tournaments demonstrate strength against frozen baselines without new collapse or rules
  pathologies.
- The player-facing CPU meets bounded latency, replay, fairness, and ordered difficulty requirements.
- Learned models, if added, predict terminal outcomes or guide search and are not treated as an
  unverified replacement for the rules/search system.

At that point the simulation policy is estimating what actions do to victory, loss, survival, and plan
completion—not what a designer once decided a city or citizen should be worth.

## 19. Research basis

- Szita, Chaslot, and Spronck,
  [Monte-Carlo Tree Search in Settlers of Catan](https://research.tilburguniversity.edu/en/publications/monte-carlo-tree-search-in-settlers-of-catan/)
  — multiplayer stochastic rollouts, domain guidance, and evidence that poorly chosen rollout bias can
  reduce strength.
- Cowling, Powley, and Whitehouse,
  [Information Set Monte Carlo Tree Search](https://pure.york.ac.uk/portal/en/publications/information-set-monte-carlo-tree-search/)
  — search over information sets for hidden information and uncertainty.
- Silver et al.,
  [A general reinforcement learning algorithm that masters chess, shogi, and Go through self-play](https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/id/eprint/10069050/)
  — evidence that self-play can bootstrap without human game records, while not directly solving
  Hegemony's multiplayer, chance, politics, and hidden-information requirements.
