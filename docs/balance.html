Everything we know about how Hegemony's numbers behave — where the economy is sound, where it is exploitable, and which knobs to turn next. Companion to rules.md (how to play), todo.md (what to build), and Hegemony.pdf (where the design is going).

How to use this ledger

Conventions, so the document stays useful as the game changes.

  • Update it with the numbers. Any change to src/game/ruleset.ts, src/game/data.ts, or the event decks should touch this file in the same commit — re-run the affected analysis, don't just edit prose.
  • Verdicts are marked inline: Strong (working as intended, protect it), Weak (unbalanced or degenerate), Watch (fine today, fragile as systems land), Drift (docs and engine disagree).
  • Issues are ranked in §XI: P0 breaks the play loop, P1 is a dominant strategy or exploit, P2 is tuning and polish.
  • Resolved items stay, struck through with a one-line note on what fixed them — the history of why a number is what it is is the value of this document.
  • Scenario seeds in §XII are the playtest backlog. Add a scenario whenever an analysis here makes a testable prediction.

IIThe resource web

Six resources, but only two of them are a real economy today. Food is the master currency; wood is the expansion currency; gold and influence are printed into a void.

Sources vs. sinks — the whole economy on one grid
ResourceSources (per turn unless noted)SinksVerdict
Food Plains tiles (4–10, map total 44) · Granary +2 · slaves on plains · event cards Pop upkeep (−1/−1/−2) · every grow-pop · colony +2 · city +5 · and the stockpile prints happiness Strong — most contested resource; nearly every decision prices in food. This is the economy working.
Wood Forest tiles (1–4, map total 33) · slaves on forest · events Colony 20 · city 30 · Marketplace/Workshop 12 · Granary 12 Strong — the tempo currency. Expansion speed ≈ wood income; forests are genuinely valuable.
Stone Mountain tiles (2–6, map total 20) · events City upgrade 10 · Temple 6 · Granary 2 Watch — thin but real. One mountain covers a whole game's stone needs; extra mountains are dead weight until Embassy/Library-class sinks (PDF: 50 and 20 stone) land.
Gold Freemen +2 · citizens +2 · hill tiles (1–4, map total 14) · events Grow citizen: 2 gold. That is the entire sink. Weak — pops out-print the whole map (3 freemen > the best hill), and there is nothing to buy. Hills are trap tiles today.
Influence Citizens +1 · Temple bonus · Patronage/Civic Petition events None. Weak — a score that buys nothing. Every influence design decision is on hold until the Assembly (PDF sinks: 5 draw / 10 exchange / 20 veto).
Happiness Temples +1 · food stockpile +1/5 · events Slaves −0.5 · overcrowding −1 · food shortage · events → unrest pop-loss thresholds Strong shape / Weak tuning — the stock-not-flow model is elegant (see §VII) but the food-stockpile faucet is uncapped.
Core imbalance

Gold and influence are faucet-only resources. Both were designed against sinks that live in the unimplemented half of the PDF — Luxury Goods' Trader (100 gold), Embassy (50 stone + 50 gold), Resolution draws (5–20 influence). Do not nerf freeman gold or citizen influence yet; the fix is adding a small interim sink, not weakening the faucet you'll need later.

Candidate interim sinks

Stabilize Province (from docs/next-mechanics.md): spend 4 influence → +3 happiness. One action, gives citizens a job before the Assembly exists. Market exchange (from the PDF): bank trade at 1:6 (coastal ports later improve to 1:4). Even at a brutal rate this un-deadens gold and stone surpluses and softens dead-tile starts.

No trade means no relief valve

There is no player trade and no bank exchange, so a player whose tiles mismatch their needs (a hill/mountain start hunting for wood) has no conversion path except event luck — the exact situation market exchange exists to solve in the PDF. Until some exchange lands, tile draw at setup is close to destiny, which puts extra weight on the placement-order problems in §V.

IIIPops

The three-class pop system is the game's best idea. Its internal pricing is close — but citizen vs. freeman collapses without an influence sink, and slaves have a hidden spatial rule that is genuinely elegant.

Per-pop economics (engine values)
PopGrow costYield / turnUpkeep / turnVPNominal payback
Slave5 food+1 tile resource (+1 more under a Workshop)−1 food, −0.5 happiness0~5 turns; ~2.5 with Workshop

Citizen9 food + 2 gold+2 gold, +1 influence (+1 inf under a Temple)−2 food1n/a — pays in two dead currencies

Citizen vs. freeman: one food apart, one class redundant Weak

As implemented, a citizen is exactly a freeman plus 1 influence, minus 1 extra food of upkeep, for a steeper grow cost. Two consequences:

  • Today (influence worthless): the citizen is strictly dominated. Never grow one except for taste.
  • Later (Assembly live, votes per-citizen as in the PDF): the citizen becomes a freeman-plus-votes and will likely strictly dominate instead — the author's own margin note in the PDF already worries that influence builds are OP.
Design question

The +2 gold on citizens is engine drift from the spec (which gives citizens influence only). Decide the citizen's identity before the Assembly lands: either the PDF's split — freeman = money, citizen = politics only (revert the +2 gold) — or lean in and make the citizen the expensive do-everything elite, priced accordingly (raise grow cost / food upkeep). The current halfway point guarantees one of the two classes is redundant in every era of the game.

The slave placement rule nobody wrote down Strong

A slave yields +1 of the tile's own resource and eats 1 food. On a plains tile that's +1 food −1 food = zero. Slaves are worthless on farmland (until a Workshop makes them +1 net) and best on high-yield forest, mountain, and hill tiles — while freemen and citizens, who don't touch the tile, are the natural farm population. The engine quietly created real spatial specialization: work camps on the resource tiles, free populations on the breadbasket. This is emergent, thematic, and worth protecting when retuning — and worth teaching in rules.md, because it is invisible until someone does the arithmetic.

Slave equilibrium

Happiness is a stock, so any steady negative flow eventually hits the −5 unrest threshold. A slave economy is sustainable only while happiness income ≥ 0:

sustainable slaves ≤ 2 × (temples + floor(stored food / 5) + net event luck)

Each Temple (6 stone) carries two slaves forever — a good, legible price. The problem is the second term: a 25-food stockpile carries ten slaves, and stockpiles only grow. See §VII.

Other pop notes

  • No voluntary pop removal. You can't disband or resettle-away a pop. Combined with random unrest removal, a player over the slave line has no lever except "stop growing and wait for the mob". Probably fine — but note the PDF's pop-removal choice rule (1 citizen = 2 freemen = 4 slaves) would double as a disband mechanic.
  • Growth is once per settlement per turn — a clean anti-snowball throttle that also makes wide (many settlements) scale growth faster than tall. Intentional? It pairs oddly with colony-spam VP dominance (§VIII).
  • The PDF's 1 citizen = 2 freemen = 4 slaves ratio (different capacity weights) was normalized to 1:1:1 in the prototype. That's a simplification with real balance content — under PDF rules 16 slaves fit where 4 citizens do, which is exactly the "+8 resources every turn" scenario the margin notes flag as broken. The 1:1:1 choice is safer; revisit only with the unrest system fully priced.

IVBuildings

Four buildings, one dominant opening. The support-cap design (buildings support N pops) is excellent; the roster is too small for paths to diverge.

Building ROI (engine values)
BuildingCostEffectBest-case paybackVerdict
Granary12 wood + 2 stone +2 food/turn · grow-pop food cost −2 here ~3 turns Weak (too good) — +2 of the master currency plus a discount you use every turn (slave drops 5→3 food). Always the correct first build; homogenizes every city's opening.
Temple6 stone +1 happiness/turn · +1 influence per citizen (≤2) n/a (buys stability) Strong — the only repeatable happiness income; cleanly prices slavery at "one temple per two slaves". Cheap on purpose, in a resource with few other uses.
Workshop12 wood +1 tile resource per slave (≤3) ~4 turns (needs 3 slaves) Strong — turns a mountain/forest city into an engine and gives plains slaves a reason to exist. Correctly demands setup (slaves in place) before it pays.
Marketplace12 wood +2 gold per freeman (≤3) 2 turns — nominally Weak — best payback in the game, into a currency with a 2-gold sink. Instantly becomes the best building the day gold matters. A pre-positioned landmine: retune it when the gold sink lands, not before.

Slots are not a constraint yet

Cities get tile slots +2 (so 3–6 slots) and there are only four buildings. Nobody ever faces a real slot decision. The map's slot spread (plains 2–4, hills up to 3, forests mostly 1) is designed to be a second axis of tile value — it only starts paying rent once the second building tier exists and a city meaningfully can't hold everything. Until then, treat slot counts as unvalidated numbers.

Second tier — design against the gaps, not the theme

The candidates in todo.md (Aqueduct +4 capacity, Forum +2 influence, Barracks, Warehouse +1 tile income) are fine, but each new building should be judged by which §-problem it solves: an influence sink building beats an influence source (Forum adds supply to a resource with no demand); Aqueduct interacts with over-capacity unrest (good tension); Warehouse must be priced against Workshop (both are "+tile income"). The PDF's Library (grants National Ideas, −2 food upkeep — the game's only upkeep building) is the most interesting shape: a building that costs food every turn is a real decision in a food-driven economy.

VMap & placement

The placement scheme has good bones — spaced city cores with colony filler — but the engine currently drops the two rules that make geography matter, and the setup sequence hands player 0 a solved opening.

What the map is worth

The spread is deliberate and mostly right: food is plentiful but concentrated (one food-10 crown jewel, one food-8), wood is everywhere but thin (expansion is slow without forest focus), stone is scarce-but-sufficient, and hills are... nine tiles of nearly nothing (see §II — gold). With ~9 tiles per player, tile quality variance between players' reachable areas is the single biggest setup swing.

Direction settled — 2026-07-11

The terrain rework is planned in docs/feat/terrain-economy.md: wood/food/stone are first-order resources (from tiles), gold/influence are second-order (from pops, trade, buildings) — gold tiles are removed, and hills become the slot-rich, yield-poor "acropolis" terrain. That plan also pins the building pricing grammar, the landmark-tile principle with constrained shuffling, the trade-before-stone-sinks sequencing rule, and luxury goods as the capped, diminishing, tradeable happiness tier. The tallies above describe the current deck and stay until the rework ships.

The two missing rules Drift

  • No colony contiguity. The PDF requires colonies within 1 tile of a friendly settlement; the engine lets you found a colony on any legal tile. Consequences: the food-10 tile is reachable by everyone from turn 1 (pure wood race), blocking and corridor play don't exist, and "your part of the island" is a fiction. This one rule change would do more for map play than any retune. Consider enforcing radius-1 from any owned settlement, with a possible later "overseas" exception via ports.
  • No capital protection radius. Both rules.md and the spec promise colonies can't sit adjacent to an enemy capital; the engine never checks. Combined with no contiguity, a rival can legally ring your capital with colonies on turn one. Either enforce it (the PDF is explicit: "Other players cannot erect Colonies in the 1-tile radius of a player's Capital!") or consciously delete the promise.

Setup order: fixed map + fixed order = solved opening Weak

Setup runs 0→1→2→3 for capitals, then 0→1→2→3 again for colonies (turn.ts), and player 0 also acts first every season. On today's identical-every-game board, player 0's optimal capital is a memorizable fact, and last-in-order players eat the leftovers twice. Three cheap fixes, stackable:

  • Shuffle the terrain deck at game creation (seeded, like the event decks) — turns the memorized opening back into an evaluation problem.
  • Snake the setup (0→3 capitals, then 3→0 colonies) — the standard fix; last capital picker gets first colony pick.
  • Rotate the season's first player each year — removes the permanent first-actor advantage on contested tiles and is thematically free (the year turns, the order turns).

Shared tiles and the eviction hammer Watch

Two colonies may share a tile at 50% yield each (floor — a food-9 tile shared gives 4 each, one food evaporates). Upgrading your colony to a city evicts the sharer outright: settlement, its pops, and the 20-wood investment are destroyed with no compensation (upgradeColonyToCity filters the other settlement away, pops included). As the only direct aggression in the game it deserves to be scary — but destroyed pops is probably over the line: the victim loses up to ~40+ resources of value to a move they cannot see coming or answer. Options short of removing the threat: displaced pops walk to the owner's nearest settlement (arriving like a normal transfer), or eviction refunds the base colony cost. Keep the tempo hit; delete the uncounterable pop kill.

What's already right Strong

  • City non-adjacency (no city next to any other city, capitals included) spaces the map into at most a handful of city cores with colony webbing between — it caps density organically, keeps the vase-grammar readable, and makes "where can a city ever exist" a real long-term planning layer. Note the corollary: your own colonies adjacent to your capital can never become cities, so careless early colonies permanently cap your city count.
  • Colony founding requires escorting a pop that spends a round in transit (arrives at your next turn) — expansion costs tempo and board presence, not just wood. Subtle and good.
  • Max two colonies per tile keeps the sharing rule bounded.

VICards & events

Two decks: a seasonal deck that gives the year a mood, and a player deck that is currently a mandatory +5-resources-a-turn slot machine.

Seasonal deck (29 cards, season-weighted draw)

Cards carry season tags; the draw takes the first tag-suited card from the shuffled pile, so seasons weight the odds without forcing outcomes — a genuinely nice mechanism (drawSeasonalCard). But look at the pools:

SummerBountiful 4 · Timber 3 · Quarry 3 · Festival 2 · Artisans 2 · Open Markets 2—0 of 16 · 0%

Finding

Spring and Summer are literally incapable of harm — the year's first half is a guaranteed boon parade, and all tension is back-loaded into Autumn/Winter. rules.md even sells this as "a tendency, not a rule", which the numbers contradict. Give Spring and Summer one thematic harm each (spring floods; summer wildfire that hits forest yields) so no season is ever auto-safe — the tendency stays, the certainty goes.

  • All seasonal cards are symmetric (hit all players), so they mostly shift the whole table rather than relative standing. The per-pop scaled cards ("2 per 6 pops, min 4") are the exception: they pay leaders more absolutely, but the minimum floor pays trailing players more per pop — a mild, well-hidden catch-up mechanism. Strong
  • Plague + Winter stacking: Plague (−2 happiness × 3 turns, everyone) drawn in Winter alongside Civic Anxiety can shove a table already carrying slave pressure straight through the −5 threshold. Symmetric in theory; in practice it executes whoever was managing happiness closest to the line. Watch in playtests whether that reads as drama or as random execution.
  • Scarce Labor / Skilled Artisans (build costs ×2 / ×½ for a season, colony & city upgrade exempt) are the best cards in the deck — they create a real "when do I build" tempo game around a public, shared modifier.

The player deck is an untuned income stream Weak

Every player draws one card per turn, mandatorily, on income collection. The deck is 74 cards:

  • ~92% upside. Only Local Unrest ×4 and Civil Discord ×2 are negative — 6 of 74 cards (8%).
  • 30% free pops. New Citizen ×8, Free Settlers ×8, Captured Laborers ×6 (+ Granary Surplus's freeman option ×3). A free citizen is ~11 resources of grow cost that also bypasses the once-per-settlement growth throttle; Captured Laborers is 2 slaves (10 food of value) for a manageable −1 happiness/turn.
  • Expected value ≈ +4.8 resource-equivalents per draw (valuing free pops at grow cost, influence near zero). That means the card slot out-earns most tiles on the island, every turn, for everyone — with a variance band from +11 (New Citizen) to −6 (Civil Discord). Over a 10-season game each player draws ~10 cards; a two-draw luck gap between players is ~±15 resources, or most of a city.
Tuning directions (pick one, not all)

(a) Cut free-pop copies roughly in half and convert them to "grow at half cost" coupons — keeps the theme, re-couples the windfall to food and capacity; (b) rebalance toward ~25% harm so a draw is a moment of tension rather than guaranteed income; (c) make the draw optional (press-your-luck) so passing is a real choice; (d) shrink amounts so the EV lands near +2, below an average tile. The PDF's own card table (Gain 1 Citizen ×6 in a much smaller deck) had roughly the same free-pop density — this is inherited, not accidental, and worth a deliberate decision.

Dominated choices on choice cards

Several pendingChoice cards have one option that's near-strictly better, which wastes the design:

  • Caravan Contacts: "gain 4 gold" vs. "exchange up to 4 wood for 4 gold 1:1" — option B is option A plus you also pay wood. Never correct. Make B a better rate (4 wood → 6 gold) or drop it.
  • Skilled Mason / Temple Donation: the flat resource option (4 stone / 3 happiness) beats the −3-stone discount in almost every state, since only Temple (6s) and Granary (2s) even cost stone. Discounts need to exceed the flat option's value to tempt.
  • Granary Surplus: "4 food or a free freeman (worth 7 food + VP)" — the freeman wins whenever capacity exists. Fine-ish (capacity is a real condition), but it's really a 7-value card wearing a 4-value option.
  • Emergency Labor (6 wood − 1 happiness vs. 2 wood) is the model: the right pick genuinely depends on your happiness ledger. More of this shape.

Decks reshuffle forever Watch

Both discard piles reshuffle endlessly, so the game has no clock and card scarcity never bites. The planned fix (finite decks; when the last card is drawn the game ends and victory cards resolve) makes deck size the game-length dial and is the single most important unbuilt mechanic — nearly every issue in §VIII is downstream of "there is no ending".

VIIHappiness & unrest

The newest system (feat/unrest-consequences) and structurally the best: a stock-based meter with real teeth. One faucet undermines it.

The model, briefly

Happiness is a running stock; income applies flows (temples, slaves, overcrowding, shortage). At the start of each turn, before income: timed modifiers tick, then thresholds fire — ≤−5 loses 2 random pops (repeats every turn until you climb), ≤−10 loses 4 and rebounds to −4; separately, food income ≤−2 on two consecutive turns starves 1 pop. No passive drift in either direction; losing pops shrinks the causes, so collapses bottom out instead of spiraling. Strong — legible thresholds, deterministic seeded removal, self-damping.

The food-stockpile faucet breaks the system Weak

+1 happiness per 5 stored food, per income collection, uncapped (foodStockpileHappinessDivisor: 5). This is the only unbounded happiness income in the game, and it converts the master currency directly into unrest immunity:

30 hoarded food → +6 happiness/turn → sustains 12 slaves forever (and the same hoard is also scoring +3 VP as banked material — see §VIII)

The hoard never depletes from providing this, so the correct line is: bank food early, then run the largest slave economy on the island with zero unrest risk. It also inverts the intended texture — food hoarding should be the famine hedge, not the happiness engine. Fixes, mildest first: cap the bonus (+2, i.e. "granaries full" as a binary comfort), tie it to income rather than stockpile (surplus feeds festivals, hoards don't), or make it a one-shot when crossing thresholds. Keep some version — "full granaries calm the city" is good theme — just not an uncapped per-turn flow.

Random removal: the punishment lottery Watch

Unrest removes pops uniformly at random across all settlements and classes. Losing 2 slaves at −5 is a slap (it even reduces your unrest causes); losing 2 citizens is ~22 resources and your influence base. Same trigger, wildly different sentence. The PDF instead lets the player choose what to sacrifice at a fixed exchange (1 citizen or 2 freemen or 4 slaves) — costlier in choice (you must keep slaves to burn), which is more interesting than costlier in luck. Worth adopting once the unrest UI can carry a choice modal; random removal is the right v1.

Remaining gaps

  • The rulebook's graded food-deficit unrest (−2/−4/−6 income → 1/2/3 unrest per turn) is deliberately not reproduced; the flat shortage-pressure + starvation counter stands in. Fine — revisit when tuning famine severity.
  • Positive happiness does nothing except buffer distance to the thresholds (and 0 VP). That's acceptable, but a small reward for high happiness (a growth discount? a VP trickle?) would make the meter a two-sided investment instead of pure insurance. The PDF's Luxury Goods (each a permanent −2 unrest ≈ +2 happiness flow) will eventually pour into the same bucket — leave headroom.
  • Fractional flows (−0.5/slave) render as half-points on the meter. Cosmetic, but a board-game-feel target argues for whole numbers (e.g. −1 per 2 slaves, rounded up).

VIIIScoring

The provisional VP formula is a placeholder — but players optimize whatever is on the scoreboard, so its distortions are already the de-facto strategy guide.

Superseded — 2026-07-12

The provisional formula analyzed below was replaced by the victory race (appendix D1, feat/phase0-victory-race): 5 public "Most X, min Y" cards, sole leader holds, 3 at your own turn start wins; the seasonal deck is the finite clock. This section stays as the record of why the formula had to go. The race's own balance questions (are the five minimums reachable at comparable speed? does Demos still over-reward width?) belong to the minimum-tuning sim campaign.

  • Colony + freeman spam is ~3× more score-efficient than city-building. Cities must earn their premium through building income compounding over remaining turns — which means the shorter the game, the more degenerate colony spam gets. With no contiguity rule (§V), colony spam isn't even geographically constrained. Weak
  • Hoarding double-dips: banked material scores VP and banked food prints happiness (§VII). Two systems independently reward sitting on resources; together they reward it twice. At minimum, exclude food from "banked material", or score only non-food materials.
  • Slaves score zero — defensible (they're infrastructure, not society), and it neatly means a slave economy must convert its output into scoring assets before game end. Keep, but be aware it interacts with the endgame trigger card "Apollo: least pops wins" from the PDF, where slaves become a liability.
  • Only negative happiness scores. +20 happiness and +1 happiness are identical on the scoreboard. Combined with the rebound-to-−4 at revolt, a calculated "controlled burn" (let the revolt fire, rebound, finish above water) is occasionally correct. Niche, but worth knowing it exists.
Design direction

The intended fix is already chosen in todo.md: finite event decks as the game clock, with victory cards (most resources / most pops / most cities / most votes…) resolving at exhaustion. That structure is inherently better than a static formula — objectives revealed over the game reward flexibility instead of a single memorized build order. The PDF's endgame triggers (Athena: most votes; Poseidon: most resources; Apollo: least pops) show the intended spread: note that Apollo is a designed hedge that punishes pure width, the exact counterweight the current formula lacks.

IXRandomness & dice

Where luck lives today, and where it should (and should not) live tomorrow. The PDF's component list ships a d6 and a d20 that no rule currently uses.

The current randomness surface

Seasonal deckSeeded shuffle + season-tag weightingStrong mechanism, mis-tuned pools (§VI).

A house philosophy for luck

Worth writing down as policy, because every future mechanic will ask the question:

  • Cards for situations, determinism for consequences. The engine's strength is that everything downstream of a revealed card is exactly calculable — Scarce Labor is fun precisely because the modifier is public and the response is skill. Keep randomness at the input edge (what the world throws at you), never at the output edge (whether your paid-for action works). Dice-to-succeed on economic actions would fight the whole design.
  • Shared randomness > private randomness. Seasonal cards (everyone suffers the drought together) create table talk and fairness; private player draws create silent inequality. The current game has the ratio backwards — the private deck is ~5× the swing of the shared one. Rebalancing the player deck (§VI) matters more than any dice question.
  • Where the d6 could honestly live: (a) unrest flavor — at the −5 threshold, roll for the mob's demand (lose pops / lose a building's use for a turn / pay a ransom), keeping severity fixed but texture varied; (b) tie-breaking and turn-order lotteries; (c) the eventual military layer the Barracks placeholder implies — combat is the one domain where output randomness is genre-expected. The d20 really only makes sense for a big event table (roll on the omen table at each new year), which could replace or season the yearly-card idea in seasons.md.
  • Catch-up should be structural, not random. The existing minimum-floors on scaled events quietly help trailing players; the PDF's Stratokles politician (at power 5, targets whoever controls him — the Catan-robber pattern, per the margin notes) is the designed leader-check. Prefer those shapes over rubber-band luck.
Adopted policy — 2026-07-11

Decks for the economy, dice for drama. Decisions from the dice spitball (now in todo.md as *** items): (1) Event tables as a reusable pattern — dice + lookup table, starting with a d6 riot table for unrest resolution: expected severity stays constant, the roll varies the texture (lose food / lose a pop / building disabled / ransom). Influence spends modify the odds — the first universal influence sink. Possible extension: the player-event card draw becomes a d20 roll on an event table; yearly/seasonal events stay card-shaped. (2) Opt-in gambles (ventures) — stake resources on a d6 for windfall / break-even / loss; chosen risk, so no feel-bad; doubles as a gold sink. (3) Payout dice rejected — a shared per-turn "market die" bonus roll was considered and turned down; base tile income stays fully deterministic. Upkeep and thresholds must never depend on a roll. (4) Riot mitigation is pre-roll only — bread, concessions, and influence are committed before the die is cast (insurance, not bribery); no post-roll fixes. (5) Riot demotion is free (the mob forces it; voluntary demotion costs influence), and engineering a riot to demote cheaply is an accepted emergent play — guarded by keeping expected riot cost above demotion value and capping it at one free demotion per riot.

The one-line reading

Most of today's "broken" resources aren't broken — they're waiting. Gold waits for the Trader and Embassy; influence waits for the Assembly; citizens wait for votes; hills wait for all three. The balance risk is sequencing: every season the prototype is played without those sinks teaches players that freemen and hills are traps, and hardens the granary-first, colony-spam meta. Interim sinks (§II) are cheap insurance while the big systems are built.

XIIPlaytest scenarios

Testable predictions from the analysis above. Play them; record whether the prediction held; feed results back into §XI. Add new scenarios whenever a section makes a claim.

  1. The Hoarder. One player banks every spare food from turn 1, grows only slaves, builds Workshops. Prediction (§VII): by year 2 they run 8+ slaves at positive happiness with zero temples, out-producing everyone, and the banked pile is also scoring VP. If the table can't punish it, issue 4 is confirmed P1.
  2. The Colony Spammer. One player never upgrades; every 22 resources becomes a new colony (anywhere on the map — no contiguity), plus freemen. Prediction (§VIII): wins the provisional score by a wide margin over a two-city builder. Compare final VP; if the gap is >30%, issue 6 stands.
  3. The Doorstep Rush. Player B founds their setup colony adjacent to player A's capital, on A's best expansion tile (legal today, contra the docs). Prediction (§V): A has no recourse and is permanently down a ring tile; the table calls it unfair. Confirms issue 3's priority.
  4. The Eviction. Two players share a food tile; one quietly saves 30 wood + 10 stone and upgrades. Prediction (§V): the victim loses colony + pops (~40 resources swing) with no warning sign visible in the UI. Measure how far behind they finish; gauge feel-bad at the table.
  5. Card-luck spread. Log every player-event draw for a full game; sum resource-equivalent value per player. Prediction (§VI): best-to-worst draw luck differs by ≥15 resources — roughly a free colony — with no decision involved.
  6. The Slave Line. A player rides exactly at the temple-sustained slave cap (2 per temple), then Winter deals Plague + Civic Anxiety. Prediction (§VI/§VII): threshold breach, 2-pop mob loss, and the loss lands mostly on non-slaves about half the time (the lottery). Note whether it read as drama or as arbitrary execution.
  7. Turn-order mirror. Same seed, two games, seat order reversed. Prediction (§V): on the fixed map the seat that picks first wins both games' setups; if outcomes track seat rather than player, issue 7 is confirmed.
